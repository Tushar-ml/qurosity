## The Question Turing Didn't Answer

In 1985, a physicist named David Deutsch wanted to know whether the laws of nature could, in principle, be reproduced by a machine. Not approximated. Not modelled. **Reproduced** — same measurement statistics, same interference patterns, same quantum behaviour.

His answer required him to invent a new kind of computer. And it left a question physics still hasn't fully settled: **is the universe computing, or just changing?**

That question sits on top of a gap Turing opened fifty years earlier. In 1936, Alan Turing proved that one abstract machine — read a symbol, write a symbol, move left or right, change state — can compute anything that is computable. What he did **not** prove is that atoms, fields, and spacetime actually obey those rules. His thesis is about **math**, not physics.

Deutsch closed the gap. Along the way, he helped invent quantum computing.

[Part 1](/posts/physics-of-compuation/landauer-principle-thermodynamic/) showed that computation is physical — every erased bit costs real heat, measured in joules. <mark>Part 2 argues the reverse: physics itself is computational.</mark> Not the opposite claim — the other direction of the same bridge. Part 1: when computers run, they obey physics. Part 2: when physics runs, it evolves state step by step under fixed rules — the same logical structure as a program. Deutsch's bet is that those two descriptions are one thing seen from two angles.

These are not the same claim. Together they are stronger: an **equivalence**. Information and physics may be two descriptions of the same underlying constraint. The universe doesn't just permit computation. In a precise, testable sense, it may *be* computation — on hardware that predates every engineer.

## Church–Turing: Math, Not Physics

In 1936, Turing and Alonzo Church asked: **what can a machine calculate?**

Turing's answer was the [Turing machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_machine) — deliberately stripped to the minimum. An endless tape of cells, each holding one symbol (0, 1, or blank). A head sits on one cell. The machine has an internal **state** — a label for where it is in the recipe. At each step it reads the symbol under the head and its current state, then follows a fixed rule: write a new symbol, move left or right, switch to a new state. Repeat until the machine halts.

That is the whole machine. No screen. No RAM. No GPU. Just a lookup table and a moving head. Turing chose this because if a problem can be solved by *any* step-by-step mechanical method, it should be solvable by *this* one — the simplest possible model of mechanical reasoning.

Church used the [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus) — a formal system for function application. Gödel and Post soon showed these models compute the same class of functions. Different formalisms, one boundary. The [Church–Turing thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis):

> If you can solve a problem with a clear step-by-step recipe, a Turing machine can solve it too.

Three catches — worth stating plainly:

**It is a belief about math, not a proven theorem.** "Recipe" is an everyday idea. A Turing machine is a precise math object. The thesis connects them; it does not prove they are identical.

**It lives on paper, not in a lab.** A Turing machine never gets hot. It erases its tape for free. Real computers — and the real universe — do not work that way. That is what [Part 1](/posts/physics-of-compuation/landauer-principle-thermodynamic/) established.

**It only asks "is it possible?" — not "how long will it take?"** Sorting three names and sorting three billion names are the same kind of problem here. One takes a blink. One takes years.

Some problems sit outside even that boundary — the [halting problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem), for example. No Turing machine can decide, for every program, whether it will stop. But all of this is still math on a page.

Deutsch later asked the harder question: **does anything in nature actually run like a Turing machine?** Not as metaphor. As matter, following rules. Turing never asked. For decades, almost nobody did.

<mark>Turing defined compute on paper. He never asked what real hardware runs the steps — or what it costs to reset the result.</mark>

## Deutsch's Physical Version

Before Deutsch, a weaker version circulated: any computer we can build can be simulated by a Turing machine. Fine for classical digital chips. It breaks down for quantum systems, field theories, and anything where the physical process itself exceeds classical simulation.

In 1985, Deutsch published [*Quantum Theory, the Church–Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis#Physical_Church%E2%80%93Turing_principles). His claim:

> Every finitely realizable physical system can be perfectly simulated by a universal quantum computer operating on a finite number of qubits.

Unpack that phrase by phrase:

- **Finitely realizable** — the system fits in a bounded region of space and time. Not an infinite universe all at once.
- **Perfectly simulated** — your model reproduces the same measurement statistics as the real system, to arbitrary accuracy. Not a cartoon.
- **Universal quantum computer** — not a faster calculator. A machine that can mimic any other physical process within that bound.
- **Finite number of qubits** — the simulation is itself a physical system with finite resources.

In plain English: **any physical process that can happen in a finite region can, in principle, be copied by a quantum computer.** Deutsch was not trying to build a better factoring machine. He was asking whether **physical law itself is computationally universal** — but only if you allow the full machinery of quantum mechanics.

What changes with Deutsch's principle:

- **Compute** is no longer "what mathematicians can write down." It is what physical law allows to evolve and be simulated.
- **Thermodynamics re-enters.** Simulation runs on real hardware. State updates can erase information. Part 1's heat bill applies to real compute steps — not abstract symbols.

## What "Simulate" Actually Means

Deutsch's principle hinges on a precise word: *simulate*.

It does not mean "rough guess on a spreadsheet." It means **reproduce the measurement statistics** of the original system to arbitrary accuracy. If you simulate a hydrogen atom, your model must predict the same spectral lines, the same transition probabilities, the same interference patterns — not a sketch of them.

The [double-slit experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment) makes this concrete. Fire electrons one at a time through two slits onto a detector screen. Each electron arrives at a single point — as if it went through one slit or the other. But over thousands of trials, an interference pattern emerges — bright and dark bands — as if each electron went through **both** slits at once.

A classical computer can store "left slit" or "right slit." It cannot efficiently store "both at once." To simulate the quantum system faithfully, you need a quantum system.

Richard Feynman saw this clearly in 1982, in [*Simulating Physics with Computers*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_complexity_theory#History). Simulating quantum mechanics on a classical machine pays an exponential price in memory and time. At its most fundamental level, the universe is not something a classical Turing machine can cheaply copy.

<mark>Simulation is not a metaphor. It is the claim that physical evolution and computation share the same boundary.</mark>

So in a physical world, to "compute" is not just to get an answer. It is to **evolve a system according to the same laws as the thing you are modeling**.

This is the heart of the post.

Part 1: erasing a bit costs at least <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} /> of heat.

This evolution is **reversible**. The full state <InlineMath expression={"|\\psi(t)\\rangle"} /> holds *all* the information. Run the clock backward and you recover <InlineMath expression={"|\\psi(0)\\rangle"} />. No hidden waste bin. No bit erased.

**Reversible means you keep the information somewhere.** In quantum mechanics, the state itself is the storage. In a reversible classical computer (Bennett's idea, Part 3), you need extra memory for every bit you would otherwise overwrite — because erasing is what costs heat.

<mark>By Landauer's principle, reversible evolution has zero thermodynamic cost — but only if nothing is forgotten.</mark>

So where does the heat come from?

From **measurement**. When you observe which slit the electron went through, a spread of possibilities collapses to one outcome. Two options become one. Information is discarded. The environment absorbs that loss as heat — exactly the mechanism from Part 1.

| Process | Reversible? | Heat cost |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Quantum state evolution | Yes | Zero |
| Measurement / decoherence | No | At least <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} /> per erased bit |
| GPU register overwrite | No | Much more than that today |

The universe, at its most fundamental level, evolves reversibly — for free. We don't, because our computers, our neural networks, our measurement apparatus all **read out and discard** intermediate results constantly.

Charles Bennett showed in 1973 that classical computation can also be made reversible in principle. Part 3 goes deeper. The insight is already here: **the heat tax is not from computing itself. It is from forgetting intermediate results.**

A forward pass through a neural network is a chain of physical state updates. Registers cleared. Activations overwritten. Each step is a miniature measurement — possibilities collapsed to one value, the rest discarded as heat. Training is worse: weeks of compute, terabytes of intermediate state thrown away between steps. Part 1's <InlineMath expression={"N_{\\text{bits}}"} /> count only makes sense once you see this: **our machines pay at every readout. The universe's basic evolution does not.**

That is why the two posts snap together. Landauer without Deutsch is a tax code with no taxpayer. Deutsch without Landauer is a universal computer with no electricity bill. Together they describe a universe that computes freely at the level of fundamental law, and charges heat only when something — a detector, a GPU, a demon — decides to forget.

<mark>Part 1 showed the universe charges heat when information is erased. Part 2 shows why — and why we pay more than nature does at the deepest level.</mark>

## Three Reasons People Push Back

Deutsch's principle is strong. It is not uncontroversial. Three objections deserve honest treatment — each opens a door the series will walk through later.

### Continuous vs Discrete

Physics is written in continuous mathematics — π never ends, smooth curves, differential equations. Turing machines operate on discrete symbols. Can a digital machine truly model a continuous universe?

The standard reply: real measurements always have finite precision. Deutsch's claim applies to what can actually be built and tested — not to perfect real numbers on paper.

But the worry lingers. If nature is truly continuous at the deepest level, maybe "digital" is the wrong frame. Part 4 takes this up.

### Penrose's Objection

Roger Penrose, in *The Emperor's New Mind* and later works, argues that human mathematical insight exceeds what any Turing machine can produce. His case runs through [Gödel's incompleteness theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems): in any consistent formal system, some true statements cannot be proved from inside the system. Penrose claims human mathematicians can see the truth of such statements — and therefore human cognition is not computable.

This is not a crackpot position. It is a direct challenge to the Church–Turing thesis itself — not Deutsch's physical extension, but the original mathematical claim.

I won't resolve it here. Part 7 will steel-man the skeptics properly; the question of whether consciousness exceeds computation deserves its own careful treatment. For now: **if Penrose is right, the physical Church–Turing thesis is the least of our worries — because the mathematical one would already be false.**

### The Planck Scale

If spacetime is discrete at the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units) — roughly 10⁻³⁵ meters — the universe may be digital at the bottom, and Deutsch's quantum computer is the right universal model.

If spacetime is continuous, or if physics permits [hypercomputation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomputation) beyond Turing machines, Deutsch's principle may be too *weak*, not too strong.

We do not know yet. But the question is empirical — testable in principle, not merely philosophical.

## What We Learned

Part 1: information is physical — measured in joules and heat.

Part 2: physics is computational — states evolve by fixed rules, simulable in principle by a universal quantum computer.

Put together, these are not metaphors stacked on metaphors. They are two descriptions of the same constraint. Shannon entropy and Boltzmann entropy share the same functional form for a reason. When your training run overwrites a billion parameters, it is not just updating weights — it is paying entropy. When an electron evolves unitarily, it is computing reversibly — for free.

| Does mean | Doesn't mean |
|-----------|--------------|
| Physical law can be simulated by a quantum computer | The universe is a video game |
| Fundamental evolution is reversible and heat-free | Every GPU op costs exactly <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} /> |
| Heat shows up when we read out and forget | Quantum computers solve every problem instantly |
| AI scaling limits are physical-process limits | Penrose is wrong (we haven't settled that) |

The mathematical Church–Turing thesis still holds in its domain. Some functions are uncomputable. The halting problem has no general solution. Deutsch does not change that.

What he changes is the frame. When we ask whether AI scaling hits a wall, we are not asking whether math runs out of theorems. We are asking whether **silicon, at room temperature, can keep erasing bits forever without drowning in heat**.

The universe computes reversibly and for free. We don't.

> **Can we close that gap — or is forgetting the price of knowing the answer?**
