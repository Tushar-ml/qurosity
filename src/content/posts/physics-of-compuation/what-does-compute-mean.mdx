## The Question Turing Didn't Answer

In 1936, Alan Turing proved that one abstract machine can compute anything computable. What he did **not** prove — what he never tried to prove — is that the real universe works this way.

His result is about **math**, not physics. It says nothing about whether atoms, fields, or spacetime follow the same rules as a paper machine. That gap sat open for nearly fifty years.

In 1985, David Deutsch closed it. Along the way, he helped invent quantum computing.

[Part 1](/posts/physics-of-compuation/landauer-principle-thermodynamic/) showed that computation is physical — every erased bit costs real heat. <mark>Part 2 argues the reverse: physics itself is computational.</mark> Not "wrong" — just the other direction. Part 1: computers obey physics. Part 2: physics evolves like a program updating state step by step. Deutsch's bet is that those two descriptions are the same thing from different angles.

## Church–Turing: Math, Not Physics

In 1936, Turing and Alonzo Church asked: **what can a machine calculate?**

Turing's answer was the [Turing machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_machine) — deliberately stripped down. An endless tape of cells, each holding 0, 1, or blank. A head reads one cell. The machine has an internal **state** — where it is in the recipe. Each step: look at the symbol, follow a fixed rule, write a new symbol, move left or right, change state. Repeat until done.

No screen. No RAM. No GPU. Just a lookup table and a moving head. If any step-by-step method can solve a problem, this bare-bones machine should be able to too.

Church used the [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus) instead — same boundary, different picture. Their conclusion, the [Church–Turing thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis):

> If you can solve a problem with a clear step-by-step recipe, a Turing machine can solve it too.

Three catches. It is a **belief about math**, not a proven theorem. It lives **on paper** — no heat, no erasure cost ([Part 1](/posts/physics-of-compuation/landauer-principle-thermodynamic/) showed real hardware is different). And it only asks **"is it possible?"** — not how long it takes.

Some problems sit outside even that — the [halting problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem), for example. But all of this is math on a page. Deutsch later asked: **does anything in nature actually run like this?** Turing never did.

<mark>Turing defined compute on paper. He never asked what real hardware runs the steps — or what it costs to reset the result.</mark>

## Deutsch's Physical Version

Before Deutsch, people assumed any computer we build could be simulated by a Turing machine. Fine for ordinary chips. Not for quantum systems.

In 1985, Deutsch claimed in [*Quantum Theory, the Church–Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis#Physical_Church%E2%80%93Turing_principles):

> Every finitely realizable physical system can be perfectly simulated by a universal quantum computer operating on a finite number of qubits.

In plain English: **any physical process in a finite region can, in principle, be copied by a quantum computer.** Not because quantum computers are fast calculators — because they are **universal simulators of physics**.

## What "Simulate" Means

*Simulate* does not mean "rough guess." It means **your model must match the real system's experimental results** — same patterns, same probabilities.

The [double-slit experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment) is the classic case. Fire electrons one at a time through two slits. Each hits one spot. But over many trials, a wave-like interference pattern appears — as if each electron went through both slits.

A classical computer can store "left" or "right." Not "both at once." Richard Feynman pointed this out in 1982: simulating quantum physics on a classical machine gets expensive fast.

<mark>Simulation is not a metaphor. Physical change and computation share the same boundary.</mark>

## Where Part 1 and Part 2 Click Together

Part 1: erasing a bit costs at least <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} /> of heat. Part 2: physical systems can be simulated by quantum computers. Now add how quantum states evolve:

This evolution is **reversible**. The full state <InlineMath expression={"|\\psi(t)\\rangle"} /> holds everything — run the clock backward and you recover <InlineMath expression={"|\\psi(0)\\rangle"} />. No bit erased. No Landauer heat.

**Reversible means you keep the information somewhere.** In quantum mechanics, the state is the storage. In a reversible classical computer (Bennett, Part 3), you need extra memory for every bit you would otherwise overwrite.

<mark>Landauer's tax hits only when something is forgotten.</mark>

So where does the heat come from? **Measurement.** Check which slit the electron took and a spread of possibilities collapses to one. Information is thrown away. The environment absorbs it as heat — Part 1's mechanism.

| Process | Reversible? | Heat cost |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Quantum state evolution | Yes | Zero |
| Measurement / decoherence | No | At least <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} /> per erased bit |
| GPU register overwrite | No | Much more than that today |

Nature evolves reversibly at the bottom — for free. We don't, because our machines **read out and discard** constantly. A GPU forward pass overwrites registers at every step. Training discards terabytes of intermediate state. **The heat tax is not from computing. It is from forgetting.**

<mark>We look at the answer and throw the rest away. That is where the bill comes from.</mark>

## Three Reasons People Push Back

Deutsch's idea is strong. Not everyone buys it.

**Continuous vs discrete.** Real numbers like π never end. Turing machines handle discrete symbols. Can a digital machine model a smooth universe? The reply: we never measure infinite precision anyway. But if nature is truly continuous at the bottom, maybe "digital" is wrong. Part 4 picks this up.

**Penrose's objection.** Roger Penrose argues human mathematicians see truths no program could prove — via [Gödel's incompleteness theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems). Serious challenge, not fringe. Part 7 will address it. **If Penrose is right, the whole story starts on shaky ground.**

**The Planck scale.** Maybe space has a smallest unit at the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units) (~10⁻³⁵ meters). If so, the universe may be digital at the bottom. If not — or if physics allows [computation beyond Turing machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomputation) — Deutsch's principle may be too weak. Experiments may settle this.

## What We Learned

- Physical processes can, in principle, be simulated by a quantum computer
- Nature's basic evolution is reversible — no heat, if nothing is forgotten
- Heat shows up when we **look at the answer and throw the rest away**
- AI scaling limits are physical, not just engineering

Not: the universe is a video game. Not: every GPU op costs exactly <InlineMath expression={"k_B T \\ln 2"} />. Not: quantum computers solve everything. Not: Penrose is settled.

Turing's thesis still holds in math. Deutsch changes the frame for physics. The AI question is not *"Will we run out of math?"* but *"Can silicon keep erasing bits without drowning in heat?"*

The universe computes reversibly and for free. We don't.
